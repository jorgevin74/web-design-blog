Discussion:

In the first reading, "The Web's Grain", the author talks about how the web has its own structural tendencies that designers usually ignore. It essentially is it's own structure and we have to put the pieces together OR restructure it and make our own blueprint for how a website is supposed to work, although if we do ignore it, it'll eventually push back, it's why we would have media queiries to try and make sure that when we shrink the size of a page down that the website also follows through and doesn't become wonky and loses its structure.

In the second reading "ChatGPT Is a Blurry JPEG of the Web", It really delves deep into the ideas of how AI takes prompts from us the user that we may have typed into google years and years and years ago. Not even google but just all around taking prompts and using them to build up CHatGPT and it sort of becomes imperfect, a sort of blurry reflection of small sections of the internet. While it could be useful to learn from it essentially isn't reliable and will not be as useful as it once was when it gets information or says information that is flat out lies and just doesn't make sense. I think that relying too much on these systems won't help us at all in the long run and instead of learning different techniques and new skills we'll eventually run to chatGPT when things get remotely hard and not even attempt to try and work things out ourselves.

In the third reading "Heavy Lies in the Digital Cloud" the author talks about how radars and sattelites were originally used to forcast storms during WW2. Soon after in the 1960s, they eventually became tools to predict climate, and helpful for everyday use and eventually the narrative of forecasts going from survivability into more for profit and strategy. Eventually this went on to how we percieve "The Cloud" or how we once percieved the cloud. Originally being a sort of archieve, now is more of a warehouse and how the model is now ready to predict the next bacthes of content ready to come into our "Cloud". It is also interesting how it has also influenced our search history and if we were to look up an item that we wanted to purchase but were on the fence, later on we would go through a rabbit hole of videos based on the idea of you buying whatever item. Something similar actually happened because I was looking through getting the Switch 2 on Amazon and literally days after my feed was just filled with Switch 2 content even though I was sort of on the fence on buying it, ended up buying it anyways since a friend said he was going to also. It sort of develops this idea of FOMO but honestly pretty happy with the purcahse so far lol.
